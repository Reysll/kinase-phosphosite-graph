Option 3 — Completed?

Our current leave-one-out (LOO) evaluation is structured exactly as Option 3.
Wefocus specifically on the 2,459 phosphosites that have two or more reported kinases in PhosphoSitePlus (cases where context should matter most). Across those sites we evaluate 6,909 kinase-site edges, comparing what the generic graph predicts vs. what the liver cancer network predicts for each site.

The pool of candidate kinases is 420.

In each Leave One Out trial (LOO), one kinase-site edge is removed from the training labels (not from the graph), the model trains on all remaining associations, and then ranks all 420 candidate kinases for that site.   
As mentioned before, I recently optimized the pipeline (graph embeddings computed once before the trial loop rather than per-trial), bringing runtime from over 24 hours down to under 5 minutes for a 50-trial set, which made the full 6,909-trial run feasible locally.

 We report two metrics because this is a multi-kinase dataset:

 - Held-out rank: the rank of the specific kinase whose edge was removed.
 - Best-true rank: for each site, the rank of the best-positioned kinase among all known true kinases for that site. If the model ranks any of the true kinases highly, that is a useful prediction even if it wasn't the one held out (Let me know if this is not right). 



For context, the random baseline for 420 candidates is ~0.24% top-1, ~4.8% top-20.

Per-trial: 2,589 of 6,909 trials (37.5%) saw an improved (lower) held-out rank in the liver model. When liver improved a trial, the average improvement was 84.9 rank positions; when it worsened, the average degradation was 65.0 positions.

As you can see, the generic model has lower mean/median rank overall. This is consistent with the streetlight effect, multi-kinase sites are dominated by well-studied hub kinases (AKT1, GSK3B, TP53) that are richly represented in the generic literature, so the generic graph naturally scores them well.

The liver model's advantage appears on the remaining ~37.5% of trials, where trial-change correlation edges provide signal that the generic graph lacks.

Results of the earlier 50-trial single-kinase debug set (for reference):

This set evaluated single-kinase sites. Generic mean rank 157.4 / median 118.5; liver mean rank 118.2 / median 104.0. Held-out rank improved in 29/50 trials (58%) in the liver model. This set is less scientifically interesting than the multi-kinase set but shows the liver model does predict better when the "correct" answer is unambiguous.

Just to be on the same page, what I understood was:

The problem with "best-true rank
  - Best-true rank would report "rank 2" (because K2 is a true kinase at position 2)
  - But K2 was not hidden, the model literally trained on the K2 edge
  - So K2 appearing high is trivial, not predictive. The model just memorized it.
  - Reporting K2's rank as a signal makes the model look better than it is

  The fix (what adjusted_held_out_rank does):
  1. Take the ranking: K7 → K4 → K2 → K1
  2. Drop all other known true kinases (K2, K3) since they had an unfair advantage
  3. New list: K7 → K4 → K1
  4. K1 is now rank 3 instead of 4

  Let me know if that was the correct interpretation.

Let me first answer the Control=Cancer=Liver FC question clearly, then compact and implement.

  Why they're equal under spectral:

  Spectral embedding computes the top-32 eigenvectors of the normalized Laplacian — this captures global community structure (which groups of nodes form dense sub-communities). All three correlation networks add edges among the same ~2,459 liver phosphosites, using the same 80th-percentile
   threshold, so they inject roughly the same number of edges into roughly the same region of the graph. At the resolution of 32 eigenvectors on 17,731 nodes, the fine-grained difference in which specific pairs are correlated (FC ratios vs healthy abundance vs tumor abundance) washes out
  entirely.

  Node2vec was sensitive to those differences because random walks are local — each node's walk distribution changes when you swap which of its neighbors are connected. Spectral is global — a perturbation that changes individual edges without restructuring communities is invisible to the
  top eigenvectors.

  Implication for the paper: The three correlation types differ at the local edge level, not the community structure level. Spectral embedding cannot distinguish them; node2vec can. The earlier node2vec result where Liver FC outperformed Control and Cancer may have captured genuine
  fine-grained signal — or it may have been degree-bias artifact. That ambiguity is now visible and worth discussing with Dr. Ayati.

---

WHAT WAS IMPLEMENTED
----------------------

Option 1 — Spectral Embedding (SpectralEmbeddingStrategy):
  I implemented normalized Laplacian spectral embedding (a way of turning each
  node's position in the graph into a vector based on overall graph structure,
  rather than on random walks) as a degree-normalizing alternative to node2vec.
  The formula is L = I - D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}, solved with scipy ARPACK for the 32
  smallest non-trivial eigenvectors. This is equivalent to what you described
  as graph contrastive learning with spectral filtering. It penalizes 
  high-degree nodes by construction, so hub kinases can no longer dominate
  the embedding purely by having many edges.
  The Node2Vec is still available if needed. I added the spectral embedding 
  rather than make it a replacement.

Option 2 — Category-Based Ranking:
  Kinases are divided into three categories based on their PSP substrate count
  (using the full generic graph, before any multi-kinase filtering):
    poor    : <= 5 substrates  (166 kinases in our 420-candidate pool)
    average : 5–20 substrates  (133 kinases)
    rich    : > 20 substrates  (121 kinases)
  I used your suggested thresholds as a starting point. For each LOO trial, the
  model now produces three separate top-1 predictions. One per category and
  ranks, the held-out kinase only against others in its own category. The
  VRK family placement: VRK3 (1 substrate) → poor, VRK2 (15) → average,
  ULK1 (77) → rich.

KSEA Implementation:
  Implemented using the Wiredja et al. 2017 formula from the paper you linked:
    score = (mean_FC_substrates - global_mean_FC) / (m * global_SD)
  where m is the number of substrates found in the dataset. 
  P-values are computed as one-tailed z-scores (how many standard deviations the score is
  from the expected mean) with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction (a standard
  adjustment that controls the false-positive rate when testing many kinases at once).

Phase 2 — Spectral Embedding (all four networks, 6,909 trials each):

  Network              | node2vec adj. median | spectral adj. median | improvement
   --------------------|----------------------|----------------------|------------
  Generic              |        188           |         79           |   -58%
  Control (healthy)    |        167           |         74           |   -56%
  Cancer (tumor)       |        174           |         74           |   -57%
  Liver FC             |        165           |         74           |   -55%

  Top-20 accuracy: generic spectral 10.6%, control/cancer/liver FC spectral all ~22%.

Two important observations from these numbers:

1. Spectral embedding roughly halves the median rank across all networks.
   This confirms the hub bias in node2vec.

2. Under spectral, Control = Cancer = Liver FC (all median 74). They were
   clearly different under node2vec (165 vs 174 vs 167). I believe
   that spectral captures global community structure (top-32 eigenvectors
   of a 16,000-node graph), so the fine-grained differences between which
   specific site pairs correlate wash out at that resolution. 
   Node2vec is local and walk-based, so it can distinguish them. The
   difference between correlation types is local, not structural.
   Generic remains the weakest (79 vs 74), confirming that correlation edges
   do add a structural signal, just not enough to separate the three types.

Category-Based Ranking:
  The category wall works as intended. Hub kinases from different categories
  no longer compete against each other. For Liver FC spectral, the within-
  category top-1 hubs are: PIK3R1 (poor, 100%), TGFBR2 (average, 100%),
  CDK1 (rich, 100%). The hubs shifted from the VRK family to nodes that are
  at least cancer-pathway-relevant (PI3K signaling, TGF-beta, CDK1).

  However, within-category hub bias remains 100% concentrated. Spectral
  embedding reduces the bias at the global level, but within each category,
  one kinase still dominates. This is the residual embedding-level bias that
  Option 1 was meant to address it helps but does not eliminate the problem.

Generic vs Liver FC — Direct Comparison (Spectral):
  I re-ran the paired generic-vs-liver comparison (same 6,909 trials, matched
  by trial) under spectral embedding. Two things stand out against the
  node2vec version of this same comparison:

  1. Top-1 prediction changes on 100% of trials (6,909/6,909) under spectral,
     versus only 23.5% under node2vec. This is because the generic spectral
     model's top-1 is dominated by TTK/IRAK4 while the liver FC spectral
     model's top-1 is dominated by PIK3R1 — two completely different hub
     kinases, so essentially every trial's top prediction flips between
     networks.

  2. Liver FC's improvement over generic is larger and more consistent under
     spectral: held-out rank improves in 64.7% of trials (vs. 56.6% under
     node2vec), and top-20 accuracy jumps from 10.6% (generic) to 22.0%
     (liver FC) versus only 2.2% to 2.7% under node2vec. So while the
     correlation-type comparison (control vs. cancer vs. liver FC) washes
     out under spectral (point 2 above), the generic-vs-liver-FC gap is
     actually more pronounced under spectral than it was under node2vec.
