1. EMBEDDING LEAKAGE FIX
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your concern:
  "I read your modification to node2vec. It seems that you calculate embedding
  once, and then in LOO, do the training. The problem is that when you have a
  link between the kinase and phosphosite, and calculate node2vec, it kind of
  keeps that link information in the embedding. Even when you remove that edge
  from your training, there is still a leakage (cheating) in the training
  (although indirect). So if you want to keep the speed and do one-time
  embedding, I have a suggestion: drop all the links between kinases and
  phosphosites that are obtained from phosphpsitePlus. calculate embedding, and
  then train and test. Don't drop edges that are obtained from other resources
  even if it is duplicate with PSP. (I forgot how we created the network, did
  we keep the site and protein level?)"

What I did:
  In leave_one_out.py, I added _build_embedding_relations() which subtracts all
  PSP 'phosphorylates' edges from the graph before calling node2vec. These edges
  remain as training labels (positive_edges). Non-PSP KSA edges are kept. This
  runs once before the trial loop, so there is no performance penalty.

  To answer your implicit question — yes, both node types are present in the
  graph throughout: PROTEIN:GENE nodes (e.g. PROTEIN:AKT1) and SITE:GENE-pSXXX
  nodes (e.g. SITE:AKT1-S473). The PSP 'phosphorylates' edges connect protein
  nodes to site nodes and are the ones stripped from the embedding graph. All
  other edge types (PPI, protein_same_pathway, has_site, site_coevolution,
  site_same_pathway, correlation edges) are retained.

Result:
  Performance dropped relative to the pre-fix numbers, as expected — the model
  can no longer indirectly read the answer via embedding structure. The corrected
  numbers are the scientifically valid ones. Importantly, the network ranking
  changed after the fix:

  Pre-fix (leaked):   Generic mean rank 140, Liver FC 149  → appeared to tie
  Post-fix (correct): Generic mean rank 190, Liver FC 176  → Liver FC consistently
                                                              outperforms Generic

  The earlier "generic wins" observation was partly an artifact of leakage
  disproportionately benefiting well-connected hub sites in the generic graph.

What I did:
  The liver graph builder (liver_network.py) now computes all three correlation

  Note on the per-pair floor: the Pearson correlation is computed per site-pair,

  the threshold filter. I have verified this at the experiment level (18/18
  samples present) but have not added an explicit assert for the per-pair count
  in liver_network.py — I can add one and log the number of dropped pairs if you
  want a hard guarantee.

  Scripts: run_multi_kinase_{generic,control,cancer,liver}.py

                          Generic   Control   Cancer    Liver FC
  Mean rank:              190.4     177.7     186.0     175.8   ← lower is better
  Median rank:            188.0     166.5     174.0     165.0

3. ADJUSTED HELD-OUT RANK (PRIMARY METRIC)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your request:
  "Best-true rank seems to be biased and suffers from leakage. what should be
  done is that, if other true kinases ranked high, just drop them from ranking,
  and then calculate the ranks. for example, let's say a substrate has 3 kinases
  of k1, k2, and k3, and you held out k1. so in the ranking you have k7, k4,
  k2, k1. So now the rank of k1 (that you held out) should be 3 instead of 4.
  Because k2 was already expected to be ranked higher since the information was
  not hidden. basically, for each ranked set, drop the true kinases, and

What I did:
  Implemented _compute_adjusted_held_out_rank() in leave_one_out.py.
  Before measuring the held-out kinase's rank, all other known true kinases for
  that site are removed from the scored list. adjusted_held_out_rank is now the
  primary per-trial metric in all scripts and the analysis summary.
  The legacy held_out_kinase_rank column is preserved for backward compatibility.
  best_true_kinase_rank (best rank any true kinase achieves, unadjusted) is also
  still reported.

6. HUB/DEGREE BIAS — CRITICAL FINDING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your note:
  "if it is ranked high in generic and liver, then it is not liver associated
  and it is mostly the network bias. If we want to remove the network degree
  bias, we can use less degree-sensitive embedding such as graph contrastive
  learning with spectral filtering."

What I observed:
  The top-1 prediction frequency across all 6,909 trials reveals a severe degree
  bias in every correlation-based network:

    Network     | Top-1 kinase (% of all 6,909 trials)
    ------------|------------------------------------------
    Generic     | ULK1 (76.5%), WNK1 (21.0%), ZAP70 (2.5%)
    Control     | VRK2 (94.8%), YES1 (4.8%)
    Cancer      | VRK3 (95.5%), VRK2 (3.8%)
    Liver FC    | ULK1 (100.0%) — every single trial

  Adding correlation edges makes one kinase so highly connected in the embedding
  graph that node2vec concentrates its representation there, and that kinase wins
  top-1 for essentially all sites regardless of biology.

  The improvements in mean/median rank metrics are real: for example, CSK moves
  from mean rank ~402 in generic to ~162 in Liver FC (100% improvement across
  all 6 of its trials). But the correct kinase never reaches rank 1 because the
  hub kinase always occupies that position.

  The adjusted rank metrics show genuine structural improvement, but biologically
  meaningful top-1 predictions will require a degree-normalised embedding. The
  EmbeddingStrategy swap architecture is already in place.

7. TTK CASE STUDY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  As discussed in my previous email, TTK has 21 trials in the multi-kinase set.
  Updated results across all four networks (post-leakage-fix):

    Network   | Adjusted mean rank | Top-1 always predicted as
    ----------|--------------------|----------------------------
    Generic   | 247                | ULK1 (18/21), WNK1 (3/21)
    Control   | 26                 | VRK2 (21/21)
    Cancer    | 113                | VRK3 (21/21)
    Liver FC  | 243                | ULK1 (21/21)

  Control dramatically improves TTK's rank (mean 26 vs 247 in generic), despite
  VRK2 monopolising top-1 every time. TTK substrates appear stably co-expressed
  in healthy liver tissue, and the control correlation edges capture that
  structural proximity effectively.

  Liver FC provides no improvement for TTK (rank 243 vs 247), suggesting TTK
  substrate fold-changes are divergent in tumors rather than co-regulated. This
  is consistent with TTK's role as a mitotic checkpoint kinase whose activity
  may be disrupted rather than amplified in tumor tissue.

9. ON YOUR TRIAL DEFINITION QUESTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your question:
  "I thought I understood the concept of trial, but then I saw your last result,
  and I think I did not understand it then. My understanding is that every time
  you remove an edge to predict, you call it trial. Then what is the meaning of
  trial per kinase? why ntrk2 has 7 trials and mapk7 has nine trials? is it
  number of substrates that they have?"

  Yes, exactly. One trial = one held-out kinase-site edge. The number of trials
  per kinase equals the number of phosphosites in the frozen multi-kinase set
  that list that kinase as one of their annotated kinases. The multi-kinase set
  requires ≥2 known kinases per site (2,459 such sites), so every trial has at
  least one other kinase annotation remaining in training. NTRK2 has 7 annotated
  multi-kinase substrates in the dataset; MAPK7 has 9.
