4. How is the proposed "inference pass" different from what we do now?
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Let me know what you think on this. Let me know if you think this is not
necessary or meaningful at all.
Two differences:

  - Held-out vs. not held-out: LOO removes one true kinase-site edge at a
    time from training and asks the model to recover it. it's a test of
    predictive accuracy against a known answer.
    The proposed inference pass trains on ALL true edges (nothing held out)
    and then, for every site, asks "if we pretend we don't know the answer,
    who would the model rank highest", there's no ground truth being
    withheld or recovered, it's a direct readout of the model's structural
    ranking. So it can't be used to claim accuracy, but it CAN be used to
    look at the full distribution of ranks every kinase gets across every
    site (which LOO can't give us, because LOO only ever evaluates the
    kinase that was actually held out).

  - Site coverage: LOO only ever ranks kinases for the 2,459 multi-kinase
    sites (because there's no "held-out edge" to test on single-kinase or
    zero-known-kinase sites). The inference pass ranks kinases for every
    site we choose to include, regardless of how many known PSP kinases it
    has, which is exactly what we need for Results 1 and 2 below, since
    those want one ranking per site across thousands of sites, not just the
    multi-kinase subset.

In other words:
LOO alone can't produce what's being asked

LOO only ever evaluates the kinase that was actually held out for a site,
and it only runs on the 2,459 multi-kinase sites in the first place
(you need a known edge to remove before you can test recovering it).
That structurally cannot give:

  - Result 1: every kinase's rank at every one of the liver-detected sites
    (you need all 420 × ~3,228 scores to build a heatmap).
  - Result 2: a full rank distribution per kinase across many sites for a
    KS-test (LOO only gives you a kinase's rank at the small number of
    sites where it happens to be a true PSP kinase, between 4 and 236
    sites per kinase in the current set, that's too few and too biased a
    sample for a real distributional comparison).

So, for either of those two results, something like the inference pass is
the way to get the data. There's no way to reconstruct it from existing
outputs (not that I catched).

5. Clarifying "3,228 liver phosphosites" vs the 6,909 LOO trials
----------------------------------------------------------------
Sorry, that was confusing.
Short answer: your concern doesn't apply, we already only predict on sites
detected in the liver data. The two numbers are just counting different
things, for different parts of the project:

  - 6,909 is not a site count. It's a count of kinase-site EDGES. A site
    with 3 known kinases contributes 3 trials (one per kinase). So 6,909
    trials come from only 2,459 sites, all of which are detected in the
    liver data and have 2 or more known PSP kinases. This is the LOO set.

  - 3,228 is a different, smaller, stricter set of sites used only for
    KSEA. It requires a clean numeric fold-change value (enough non-missing
    measurements in both tumor and control). It has nothing to do with PSP
    kinase counts. Many of those 3,228 sites have zero known kinases, and
    many sites used in LOO don't have a clean enough FC value to be in this
    set. That is why the numbers don't line up, they are not built from the
    same filter.

So nothing needs to change about the LOO methodology. The only place the
3,228-site definition matters is Results 1 and 2 below, where we do want to
restrict to sites with valid liver FC data, per your reasoning.
