1. EMBEDDING LEAKAGE FIX
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your concern:
  When node2vec sees a kinase-phosphosite link during embedding, the structural
  information about that relationship is captured in the node vectors even when
  the edge is withheld as a training label — an indirect form of data leakage.
  You suggested dropping all PhosphoSitePlus (PSP) KSA edges before computing
  embeddings, while keeping non-PSP KSA edges from other resources even if they
  coincide with a PSP annotation (since the label being predicted is specifically
  the PSP annotation).

What I did:
  In leave_one_out.py, I added _build_embedding_relations() which subtracts all
  PSP 'phosphorylates' edges from the graph before calling node2vec. These edges
  remain as training labels (positive_edges). Non-PSP KSA edges are kept. This
  runs once before the trial loop, so there is no performance penalty.

Result:
  Performance dropped relative to the pre-fix numbers, as expected — the model
  can no longer indirectly read the answer via embedding structure. The corrected
  numbers are the scientifically valid ones. Importantly, the ranking of networks
  changed after the fix:

  Pre-fix (leaked):  Generic mean rank 140, Liver FC 149  → appeared to tie/reverse
  Post-fix (correct): Generic mean rank 190, Liver FC 176  → Liver FC consistently
                                                              outperforms Generic

  The earlier observation of "generic wins on multi-kinase hub sites" was partly
  an artifact of leakage disproportionately benefiting well-connected hub sites
  in the generic graph.

6. HUB/DEGREE BIAS — CRITICAL FINDING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your prediction:
  If a kinase is ranked high in both generic and liver networks, that is likely
  a network degree bias rather than liver-specific biology. You noted that node2vec
  is degree-sensitive, and suggested graph contrastive learning with spectral
  filtering as a remedy.

What I observed:
  The top-1 prediction frequency across all 6,909 trials reveals a severe degree
  bias in every correlation-based network:

    Network     | Top-1 kinase (% of all 6,909 trials)
    ------------|------------------------------------------
    Generic     | ULK1 (76.5%), WNK1 (21.0%), ZAP70 (2.5%)
    Control     | VRK2 (94.8%), YES1 (4.8%)
    Cancer      | VRK3 (95.5%), VRK2 (3.8%)
    Liver FC    | ULK1 (100.0%) — every single trial

  Adding correlation edges makes one kinase so highly connected in the embedding
  graph that node2vec concentrates its representation there, and that kinase wins
  top-1 for essentially all sites regardless of biology.

  The improvements in mean/median rank metrics are real: for example, NTRK2 moves
  from rank ~380 in generic to rank ~63 in liver FC (100% improvement across all
  7 of its trials). But none of the correlation networks ever recovers top-1
  biology because the hub kinase always occupies that position.

  This is exactly the phenomenon you anticipated. The adjusted rank metrics show
  genuine structural improvement, but biologically meaningful top-1 predictions
  will require a degree-normalised embedding. The EmbeddingStrategy swap
  architecture is already in place.

  checkpoint kinase whose activity pattern may be disrupted rather than amplified
  in tumor tissue.

9. ON YOUR TRIAL DEFINITION QUESTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Your question: "why does NTRK2 have 7 trials and MAPK7 have 9 trials?"

  One trial = one held-out kinase-site edge. The number of trials per kinase
  equals the number of phosphosites in the frozen multi-kinase evaluation set
  that have that kinase as one of their annotated kinases. The multi-kinase set
  requires ≥2 known kinases per site (2,459 such sites), so every trial is a
  held-out edge from a site where at least one other kinase annotation remains
  in the training set. NTRK2 has 7 annotated multi-kinase substrates in the
  dataset; MAPK7 has 9.
