abstract

Protein phosphorylation regulates cellular signaling and cancer progression, yet more than 90% of experimentally identified phosphosites lack annotation of their upstream kinases. Existing computational approaches for kinase–substrate association (KSA) prediction are biased toward well-studied kinases, a phenomenon often described as the streetlight effect. This imbalance limits discovery of understudied kinases that may play critical roles in disease.

We developed a large-scale heterogeneous protein–phosphosite network integrating biochemical, pathway, interaction, and evolutionary evidence. To mitigate annotation bias, we propose a stratified modeling strategy in which kinases are grouped by annotation density, enabling fairer prioritization within comparable categories.

The resulting network contains approximately 13.6k nodes and over 1 million edges. Structural analysis reveals heavy-tailed connectivity consistent with annotation imbalance. Integration of liver cancer phosphoproteomic and proteomic correlations demonstrates feasibility of incorporating context-specific molecular information. These results establish a scalable baseline for bias-aware and context-specific KSA prediction.


OUR Hypothesis

Our hypothesis is that integrating heterogeneous biological data within a network framework, combined with stratified modeling, can mitigate bias toward well-studied kinases and provide fairer prioritization of understudied kinases avoiding the “Streetlight” effect.
Annotation imbalance in phosphorylation data produces structural bias toward well-studied kinases.
Stratifying kinases by annotation density enables fairer prioritization of understudied kinases.
Integration of liver cancer–specific molecular correlations enhances context-aware KSA modeling.

METHODS

Heterogeneous Network Graph Representation
The heterogeneous graph contains two node types: proteins and phosphosites. Kinases and phosphatases are represented as proteins; their functional roles are encoded in edge types. This design preserves biological identity and ensures that all interactions involving a protein contribute to a unified network representation.
Edge types include:
• Protein → Site: phosphorylates / dephosphorylates
• Protein → Site: has_site
• Protein ↔ Protein: shared pathway and high-confidence interaction
• Site ↔ Site: shared pathway and co-evolution
Context-Specific 
Liver cancer phosphoproteomic and proteomic datasets were used to compute Pearson correlations separately for normal and tumor samples. Frequency-normalized histograms were generated to compare global correlation distributions across conditions. These correlation will be integrated to the network. 

Figure 3. Global topological properties of the integrated heterogeneous network. The graph contains over one million edges with low density characteristic of biological systems and forms a single connected component, indicating successful multi-layer integration.

RESULTS
Global Correlation Structure in HCC
We computed pairwise Pearson correlations separately within normal and tumor samples (minimum 10 samples per pair). Across normal samples, both proteomics and phosphoproteomics exhibit heavier positive and negative correlation tails, indicating tightly coordinated molecular programs. In contrast, tumor samples show reduced extreme correlations, suggesting disruption of highly coordinated protein and phosphorylation relationships. These findings indicate that liver cancer perturbs strongly co-regulated molecular interactions, leading to loss of functional coordination.

Figure 4. Protein correlation distributions in normal and HCC samples.
Frequency-normalized histograms of Pearson correlation coefficients computed from HCC proteomic data. Overlayed distributions enable direct comparison of global co-expression structure between normal and tumor samples.

Figure 5. Phosphosite correlation distributions in HCC phosphoproteomics.
Phosphorylation patterns show a similar shift in correlation structure between conditions. Reduced tail magnitude in tumor samples suggests altered coordination of phosphorylation-dependent regulatory processes.

CONCLUSIONS
This study establishes a scalable heterogeneous network framework that capture different kind of relationships among kinases, phosphatases, phosphoproteins and phosphosites. Structural analysis confirms annotation imbalance consistent with the streetlight effect and supports the need for bias mitigation strategies. Stratified modeling provides a principled mechanism for fairer kinase prioritization. Integration of liver-specific phosphoproteomic and proteomic correlations demonstrates feasibility of constructing context-aware networks capable of capturing dynamic signaling relationships. Together, these results provide a practical foundation for bias-aware, cancer-specific KSA prediction in liver cancer as a future work.
